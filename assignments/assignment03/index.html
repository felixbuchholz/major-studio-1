Climbing the Energy Ladder – A Way out of Poverty?

Using dirty fuels has devastating effects on health. A detriment especially the already most vulnerable communities and individuals can’t opt out of when they want to meet their most basic needs: heating, cooking and lighting.

Immediately the image of a vicious circle comes to mind, when thinking about the consequences of respiratory health issues dramatically diminishing the possibilities to participate in society and economy.

The Energy ladder is a model for the idea that with growing socio-economic status, people can (and will) choose cleaner fuels to satisfy their energy needs. Primitive fuels like firewood are substituted by transitioning fuels like kerosene and coal to finally reach the top of the ladder: LPG, electricity and biofuels.

I want to use this model but change the perspective: Can higher access to clean fuels help reducing poverty?

How can we look at this from a data perspective?

Poverty is a complex societal issue, so there are a multitude of perspectives to look at and ways to measure it.

A first one is to look at a single and uniform indicator. The Worldbank introduced a threshold of 1.25 int. $ PPP a day. It was updated in 2015 to 1.90 a day. – This measure offers the opportunity to directly juxtapose it with the development of access to clean fuels in a single graph.

Another method would be to look at the context of a country, the state of its development and its own national poverty line to try and offer perspectives on how different changes over time could be related.

We’ll start with this.
